# 4. How to Spot AI-Generated Content

*Educational explainer · CC BY 4.0 · AI Knowledge Commons*

As generative AI becomes common, a key part of literacy is learning to tell
human-written text and media from machine-generated output — not to be paranoid,
but to think critically about what you read and share.

> This article does not promise a foolproof detector. AI is improving fast, and
> "tells" that work today may not work tomorrow. The goal is **healthy skepticism**,
> not certainty.

## Why it matters

- **Misinformation**: synthetic text can spread false claims at scale.
- **Authorship & trust**: knowing who (or what) produced a piece helps you weigh it.
- **Your own work**: if you use AI to draft, you should understand how to disclose it honestly.

## Signals in AI-written text

None of these *proves* AI authorship, but clusters of them raise a flag:

- **Uniform structure**: every paragraph the same length, every section a tidy
  "Here are three points" list.
- **Fluff and hedging**: lots of "In today's fast-paced world…", "It's important to note…",
  "a double-edged sword", "navigating the landscape".
- **Vague authority**: claims stated confidently but with no source, date, or name.
- **Repetition**: the same idea restated with synonyms instead of advanced.
- **Flat specifics**: numbers that are suspiciously round or unsourced.

## Signals in AI-generated images

- **Hands and fingers**: too many, fused, or bent wrong.
- **Text in images**: gibberish words, missing letters, nonsensical signs.
- **Teeth and eyes**: uneven, extra, or oddly spaced.
- **Backgrounds**: melting architecture, duplicated patterns, impossible reflections.
- **Lighting**: inconsistent shadows or highlights across the subject.

## What to do instead of guessing

1. **Check the source.** Who published it, and do they link original evidence?
2. **Use detection as a hint, not proof.** Detector tools have high error rates.
3. **Look for provenance.** Some platforms embed C2PA metadata marking AI origin.
4. **Ask the author.** If it matters, the honest answer is the most useful signal.

## A short self-check

Before sharing something, ask: *Do I know who made this, and on what evidence?*
If not, label it as unverified and move on. That single habit defeats most harm.

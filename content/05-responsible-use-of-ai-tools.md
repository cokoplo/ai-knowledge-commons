# 5. Responsible Use of AI Tools

*Educational explainer · CC BY 4.0 · AI Knowledge Commons*

AI assistants are powerful, but like any tool they can help or harm depending on
how you use them. This guide covers practical habits for using them well.

## Treat AI as a collaborator, not an oracle

Models predict plausible text — they do not *know* truth. Verify important claims
before you act on them, especially for:

- Health, legal, or financial decisions
- Code you will deploy
- Facts you will publish or cite

## Protect privacy

- Do **not** paste secrets, passwords, private keys, or personal data you would not
  post publicly.
- Remember that your prompts may be stored by the service you use.
- When in doubt, anonymize examples before sharing them with a model.

## Be honest about authorship

- If you used AI to draft something, say so where it matters.
- Don't present machine output as your own original work in contexts that require
  originality (schoolwork, journalism, art contests) unless the rules allow it.
- Cite sources the model gives you only after you have checked them — models
  invent citations.

## Use it to learn, not to skip learning

- Ask the model to *explain*, not just to *answer*.
- Work a problem yourself first, then compare.
- Use generated code as a starting point you read and understand, not a black box.

## Reduce waste and bias

- Smaller, specific prompts get better results than vague walls of text.
- Watch for biased or stereotyping output; models reflect biases in their training data.
- If output is wrong or harmful, say so — feedback improves the ecosystem.

## A one-line rule

> **Use AI to extend your judgment, never to replace your responsibility.**
